During the recent AM Rally I met on the air some super people. The
amateur radio operators who were willing to take a few minutes to help
me improve my audio, my techniques, and share a bit of life through
stories.

These ***Beacons*** were interested in Elmering AM-newbies and in
passing on the legacy behind the mode and their gear to new hams. They
spoke in one case of a person's AM-Journey and how operating AM would
make you a better story teller and introduce you to what they saw as the
golden age of hardware and of course the AM mode.

They were about sharing, coaching and having fun. Yes, truly ***Beacons***!

Then I also found enclaves of operators who first refused to acknowledge
breakers and then retreated to 160m AM for a multi hour long whining
session about AM Rally participants, just because they imagined that the
AM Newbies wouldn't "have the iron" to do 160m.

***Autosaboteurs*** --- an uncommon word for those who actively undermine
themselves and others.

These ***Autosaboteurs*** spoke in long monologues how the AM Rally newbies
were an affront to ham radio, and how the Rally was violating their
expectations of insularity and isolation.

I heard them describe how heading to 160m was a purposeful effort to
shun the AM Rally folks, and how much they hated breakers.

It was a lot more than "I don't want to do the AM Rally, so I got
out of the way."

Truthfully they were just on the edge of being outright nasty, but they
did make it clear new AM Hams were not welcome, as they saw them as
"pretend hams."

I heard one mock things that I had been saying in appreciation in AM
QSOs on other bands.

Some spoke of not wanting to end up in some AM Rally operator's log,
so they would ignore breakers and calls from people who weren't in
their known group.

One fellow spoke of how his ham shack full of ex-broadcast gear would
become his tomb, suggesting that when he died they could leave him there
and fill the place with concrete.

So he sure didn't plan to develop, nurture and leave any legacy. His
amazing ham experiences would be matched with a a Viking funeral, where
the most valued tools would be not passed on, but would be destroyed at
his death.

Truly it boggles how some people want to share the ham experience while
their fellows want to horde that experience as something never to be
shared.

Planning to be entombed with your heavy iron likely was tongue-in-cheek,
but the running away with the ball hiding from newer hams remains a
character flaw.

The stories on how unless you were a techie at a commercial radio
station well before the time you were weaned off of mother's milk,
that lacking that you could never be a ham like them, well that is
classic BS.

We all have different life journeys, different winding ways we got to
being interested in hm radio.

The ultra-vintage tech-from-birth is just another way to play the
interpersonal game of "topper", this time by exclusionary
requirements.

It lacks the depth and wisdom to listen hard to other's story, to
their "personal legend" as they would say in olden days, and
respectfully first accept it for what it is. Secondly you listen to
learn.

The ***Autosaboteurs*** have decided to largely pause learning, lest anyone
gets ahead of where they are at.

I think they end up missing the joy of seeing someone you are mentoring
excel beyond your skills --- that amazing taking it further, father,
higher!

On a more pragmatic level these ***Autosaboteurs*** diminish the value of
their carefully restored collections. If there are no younger hams
interested in heavy metal radios and likewise given the skills to safely
work on them, well those magnificent heavy metal stations are just as
likely to end up recycled than cherished.

As these have metal stations take a lot of room, require dedicated
wiring, can be massively heavy to move, need specialist spares, and
on dozens of other way demand a lot more from the ham than the 18 Lb
all-in-one FlexRadio Aurora which plugs into a standard wall socket,
the younger ham has to really be cultivated to carry on with the heavy
metal.

The lack-of-new-hams demand drop for many types of boat anchors is
a fraction of the likely dropping in demand the heavy iron crew is
cultivating.

That is expect for the ***Beacons*** --- as in the midst I did meet
on air a handful of very enthusiastic hams. I could tell they were
instinctively generous and ready-to-Elmer.

The contact with these ***Beacons*** left me wondering if I got a Collins 20V2
could I to get on the air with heavy metal radios? Maybe I could clear
enough space to make room for some big gear?

Yes, these ***Beacon*** types could inspire a person into joining their
interest area in the hobby.
